How could you differentiate between heavy versus unsafe nursing assignments? (Identify and discuss 3 examples)

Correct answers:

  1. It may be just a heavy assignment if you are still able to give care to your assigned patients ensuring no medically necessary care is missed (e.g., medications are given, dressings are changed, people are fed, etc.). However, you may be unable to give complete baths/showers or change bedding.
  2. It may just be a very heavy assignment if you do not get a break and/or only get a limited lunch. If, however, you cannot leave your assigned floor at all, or even take time for a biological break, this situation may be bordering on an unsafe assignment. Discuss what is happening as soon as possible with your charge nurse.
  3. It may be an unsafe work environment if one or more of your patients is having a medical emergency and this will not allow you to care for other patients assigned to you. This situation must be reported to the charge nurse so that help can be provided to ensure patient safety.
  4. The judgement of whether an assignment is heavy or unsafe will depend on the acuity of the patient assignment. If many of your assigned patients are of a high acuity, you have limited help, and you believe patients will be harmed because you cannot safely care for them, it is your right to challenge the assignment.

Who do you talk to and in what order if you are concerned with your nursing assignment?

Person Your Answer (1-6)
Charge Nurse
Nurse Manager
Unit Director or VP
Your Union Rep
Ministry of Labour
Your workplace President and CEO

The correct order is typically: 1. Charge Nurse, 2. Nurse Manager, 3. Unit Director or VP, 4. Your Union Rep, 5. Ministry of Labour, 6. Your workplace President and CEO.

Rationale: Always start with the person who is in charge and closest to patient care. The Charge Nurse will want to know why you think your assignment is unsafe. If you explain it and they are unconvinced, you may need to escalate it to the next level, which is the Unit Manager. If the Unit Manager does not provide you with an adequate response the next step is to contact the Unit Director/VP. Next, you should report the issue to your union rep but know that this process may take time, and they cannot usually help you in an urgent situation. If you believe that your assignment is truly unsafe and will cause patient harm, you can refuse the work assignment and file an official complaint with the Ministry of Labour. Some organization Presidents and CEOs are readily available to call but usually they will refer you back to your Unit manager/director or VP.

How could you start a conversation about your workload with a charge nurse?

Come prepared, list your concerns, and be ready to discuss your issues. Listen to what the person has to say in response, keep your tone respectful, and be polite and calm. If you do not feel that you are getting what you need, be prepared to escalate your concerns to the next level. Let the person know if you are going on to the next level.
